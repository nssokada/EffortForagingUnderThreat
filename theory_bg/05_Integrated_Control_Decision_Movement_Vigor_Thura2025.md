# The Integrated Control of Decision and Movement Vigor

**Source:** Thura, D., Haith, A. M., Derosiere, G., & Duque, J. (2025). The integrated control of decision and movement vigor. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 29*(12), 1146–1157.

---

## Core Claim

Decision vigor (speed of deciding) and movement vigor (speed of moving) are **co-regulated by default**, even when this does not benefit task utility. When co-regulation becomes counterproductive, they can be **decoupled** — but at a cost to accuracy. The paper proposes two complementary neural mechanisms: **signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) modulation** in sensorimotor areas underlies default co-regulation, while **inhibitory control** via frontal areas and the basal ganglia hyper-direct pathway enables flexible decoupling.

---

## Key Concepts

### Vigor
- **Decision vigor:** Speed at which a decision is made (related to steepness of evidence accumulation).
- **Movement vigor:** Speed of the movement that registers the choice (e.g., reaching velocity, saccade speed).

### Utility
The subjective value of a goal arising from the balance between costs (time, effort) and rewards. The **rate of reward** is a key utility metric: value of rewards minus effort costs, divided by time.

### Speed–Accuracy Tradeoff (SAT)
The inverse relationship between response speed and accuracy. Two distinct phenomena:
- **SAT navigation:** Adjusting speed and accuracy within the existing tradeoff curve (faster = less accurate, or vice versa).
- **SAT shift:** Changing the tradeoff curve itself (faster responses with no accuracy cost, or even improved accuracy — typically at higher metabolic cost).

---

## Two Modes of Coordination

### 1. Co-Regulation (Default Mode)

Decision and movement vigor rise and fall together. This appears to be the **default** coordination pattern, occurring even when not explicitly beneficial for utility.

**Evidence:**
- In the "tokens task" (dynamic evidence accumulation + reaching), high urgency increases both decision speed and movement speed.
- Instructing faster decisions leads to faster movements; instructing faster movements accelerates decision-making.
- Importantly, co-regulation often involves a **shift** in the decision SAT — decisions are faster without losing accuracy, suggesting the brain enhances processing efficiency globally rather than simply trading accuracy for speed.
- Stroke patients show correlated changes in reaction time and movement speed under time pressure.

**Key insight:** Co-regulation may be a product of evolutionary optimization that has become a default circuit property, activating whether or not immediate utility justifies it.

### 2. Decoupling (Flexible Override)

Decision and movement vigor can be independently adjusted when co-regulation would be counterproductive.

**Evidence:**
- When motor constraints demand slow, accurate reaching (small target), decisions become faster — but also less accurate (SAT navigation, not shift).
- When movements can be fast, decisions slow down and improve in accuracy.
- In monkeys, the coordination mode can evolve within an individual from co-regulation to compensatory (decoupled) mode based on experience.
- Rats on a treadmill show co-regulation when reward utility changes gradually, but decoupling under abrupt reward/cost manipulations.

**Key insight:** Decoupling typically involves **navigation within a fixed SAT** — prioritizing speed in one domain forces accuracy costs, which are compensated by slowing down in the other domain.

### Summary of Modes

| Mode | Decision speed | Movement speed | Decision accuracy | SAT mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Co-regulation (high vigor) | Fast | Fast | Maintained or improved | SAT shift |
| Co-regulation (low vigor) | Slow | Slow | Maintained or degraded | SAT shift |
| Decoupling (fast decision, slow movement) | Fast | Slow | Typically reduced | SAT navigation |
| Decoupling (slow decision, fast movement) | Slow | Fast | Typically improved | SAT navigation |

## Neural Mechanisms

### Mechanism 1: SNR Modulation → Co-Regulation

**Signal-to-noise ratio enhancement** in sensorimotor areas underlies the default co-regulation of decision and movement vigor.

**How it works:**
- Enhanced SNR means more targeted recruitment of task-relevant neurons relative to task-irrelevant ones.
- For movements: better neural signal → more precise motor commands → faster, more accurate movements.
- For decisions: in the embodied cognition framework, decisions are encoded in the same motor structures. Enhanced SNR increases the ratio between choice-related and choice-unrelated neural activity, accelerating the accumulation of evidence for one action option to reach threshold sooner.
- SNR enhancement may also extend to sensory areas, improving efficiency of evidence processing.

**Neural evidence:**
- Preparatory suppression and surround inhibition in motor cortex (measured via TMS) accompany faster decisions and reaction times.
- Fast decisions in the tokens task are associated with deeper inhibition of non-agonist muscles.
- Both preparatory suppression and surround inhibition are diminished in Parkinson's disease — characterized by both bradyphrenia (slow thinking) and bradykinesia (slow movement).

**Mediating circuits:**

1. **Locus coeruleus–noradrenaline (LC-NA) system:**
   - Projects broadly across the brain; well-positioned for global modulation.
   - Activity parallels demands on decision speed, movement vigor, and effort encoding.
   - Creates "hotspots" of activity — winner-takes-more, loser-takes-less — effectively reducing task-irrelevant noise.
   - Non-invasive LC stimulation enhances decision SAT (improved accuracy without slowing).

2. **Basal ganglia–dopamine (BG-DA) system:**
   - Striatum modulates gain of sensorimotor circuits, directly impacting SNR.
   - Striatal and BG output activity is amplified with increases in both decision and reaching speed.
   - DA is established in mediating motivation-related invigoration of both decisions and movements.

### Mechanism 2: Inhibitory Control → Decoupling

When SNR-driven co-regulation is counterproductive, the **hyper-direct pathway** (frontal cortex → subthalamic nucleus) enables independent adjustment of decision speed.

**How it works:**
- The hyper-direct pathway induces global suppression of motor activity, effectively raising the decision threshold.
- This allows decision accuracy to take precedence over speed, even when the motor system is invigorated for fast movements.
- The STN shows temporally separate correlations with decision threshold and movement vigor — enabling adaptation of both together while retaining flexibility to adjust one independently.

**Neural evidence:**
- TMS studies show globally reduced leg muscle activity during slow (vs. fast) hand decisions in the tokens task — suggesting recruitment of inhibitory control to extend deliberation.
- STN deep-brain stimulation in humans shows temporally separate control of decision threshold and movement vigor within the same nucleus.
- STN DBS can increase impulsiveness, consistent with disruption of the inhibitory signal that normally allows cautious decisions.

## Embodied Cognition Framework

The theory adopts an embodied cognition perspective:
- Decisions are not computed abstractly and then handed off to the motor system.
- Evolving decisions are continuously reflected in the state of the motor system — in the firing rates of neurons in areas that plan movements.
- Movement costs influence decisions; reward expectations shape motor preparation.
- This interdependence is a fundamental property of sensorimotor circuits, not an incidental coupling.

This framework makes co-regulation **natural** rather than puzzling: if decisions and movements share neural substrates, global SNR modulation will inherently affect both.

## Clinical Implications

### Parkinson's Disease
- **Bradykinesia + bradyphrenia** may reflect disrupted SNR modulation due to dopaminergic (and possibly noradrenergic) degeneration.
- Diminished preparatory suppression and surround inhibition in PD patients support reduced SNR.
- STN dysfunction may additionally impair the flexibility to decouple decision and movement vigor when needed.
- Motor symptoms (rigidity, variability, slowness) may also reduce the economic utility of actions, creating a reinforcing cycle of reduced motivation and vigor.

### Impulsivity
- Not simply "too much vigor" — rather, a failure of inhibitory control mechanisms that normally allow decisions to be regulated independently of movements.
- Impulsive individuals may be unable to flexibly shift the SAT toward more cautious behavior.
- STN DBS can increase impulsiveness by disrupting the cortico-STN inhibitory signal.

### Diagnostic Implications
- Weak joint invigoration of decisions and movements → BG- and/or LC-related deficits.
- Difficulty decoupling decision and movement vigor → STN dysfunction / impaired inhibitory control.
- These distinctions could be identified through behavioral tasks and could improve diagnosis and treatment.

## Outstanding Questions

1. How do motor vigor instructions influence SNR in visual/sensory areas during visuomotor tasks?
2. How do LC and BG contribute to and interact in regulating sensorimotor SNR adjustments?
3. What is the precise relationship between coordination mode (co-regulation vs. decoupling) and SAT dynamics?
4. What roles do serotonin and acetylcholine play in effort allocation and vigor regulation?
5. How is the compromise between effort, time, and reward resolved within and between individuals? Does this tradeoff evolve over a lifetime or differ across species?
