# Movement Vigor as a Reflection of Subjective Economic Utility

**Source:** Shadmehr, Reppert, Summerside, Yoon & Ahmed (2019). *Trends in Neurosciences*, 42(5), 323–336.

---

## Central Thesis

Movement vigor — how fast and how quickly we initiate a movement — serves as a real-time, continuous proxy for the brain's subjective economic evaluation (utility) of an expected outcome. The same neural computations that drive what we choose also shape how we move toward the chosen option.

---

## Key Definitions

- **Utility:** The subjective valuation of an outcome — how much one values a particular good.
- **Vigor:** The reciprocal of total movement time (reaction time + movement time), normalized for distance. A faster, more promptly initiated movement = higher vigor.
- **Capture rate:** (Total rewards acquired − total efforts expended) / total time. A fitness-relevant currency that links decisions and movements.
- **Marginal value theorem:** An optimal foraging policy stating that movement and decision control require comparing local reward/effort rates against the global average rate experienced in the environment.

---

## Why Vigor Matters for Measuring Utility

Traditional utility measurement relies on **choice data**, which has two limitations:

1. Choice data yields only **ordinal** preferences (A > B), not a numeric scale of how much more A is preferred.
2. Choice lotteries (von Neumann–Morgenstern) attempt to assign numeric utility, but require the assumption that people represent probabilities objectively — which they do not (Kahneman & Tversky's prospect theory).

Vigor offers a **continuous, real-valued** behavioral measure that can quantify the *degree* of preference, not just the *ordering*.

---

## Core Theoretical Claims

### 1. Reward Increases Vigor

- Saccadic eye movements are faster and initiated sooner toward rewarding targets.
- Reaching movements are faster toward rewarded directions and preferred items.
- Probabilistic reward modulates vigor via expected value: higher expected value → faster saccades, shorter reaction times.
- Real-world implication: we walk faster toward a friend we value than toward someone we don't.

### 2. Effort Decreases Vigor

- Greater target distance (more effort) → longer reaction times for both saccades and reaches.
- Greater required force or duration → longer reaction times.
- Heavier limb mass directions → slower reaches.
- Steeper inclines or added load → slower preferred walking/gait speeds.
- Under constant reward, the brain selects longer latencies and lower speeds for high-effort movements.

### 3. The Link: Capture Rate Maximization

Both choices and movements contribute to a single currency — the **capture rate**. Because:

- Movements cost effort (energy expenditure).
- Decisions determine reward accumulation.

A policy that maximizes the capture rate naturally coordinates motor control with decision-making.

The **marginal value theorem** formalizes this: the optimum movement duration occurs when the average rate of effort during the movement equals the negative of the average capture rate. Thus:

- High-utility environment → move faster (increased vigor), harvest less at each patch.
- Low-utility environment → move slower, linger longer.

### 4. Vigor During Deliberation

During active decision-making between options (e.g., choosing between an immediate small reward vs. a delayed large reward):

- Saccade speed is elevated during the deliberation period and drops after the decision.
- The last saccade before a choice is faster if directed toward the preferred option.
- The difference in saccade speed between options correlates with the difference in their subjective utility.

This makes vigor a **real-time readout** of the ongoing evaluation process.

---

## Between-Subject Differences in Vigor

- Healthy individuals show stable, trait-like differences in vigor across eye, head, and arm movements.
- These differences are **not** explained by biomechanics alone or by speed-accuracy tradeoffs (fast movers are equally accurate).
- Individual differences in vigor correlate with willingness to exert effort: those who value time more highly move faster.
- Vigor may reflect individual differences in dopamine signaling in the striatum and prefrontal cortex.

---

## Age-Related Changes

- Walking speed declines with age; metabolic cost of walking at a given speed increases with age.
- Mitochondrial capacity, muscle efficiency, and aerobic capacity all covary with preferred walking speed in older adults.
- Temporal discounting rates are highest in teens, then decline — loosely mirrored by saccade velocity peaking in teens and declining thereafter.
- The increased objective cost of movement likely contributes to reduced vigor in aging.

---

## Neural Substrates

- **Cortex:** Parietal (LIP) and frontal (OFC, FEF) neurons encode subjective value; higher discharges for higher utility lead to faster rise-to-threshold and greater vigor.
- **Basal ganglia:** Striatum, globus pallidus, and substantia nigra encode both utility and vigor. Manipulation of these regions causally modulates vigor.
- **Dopamine:** Dopaminergic transmission in the basal ganglia is central. Parkinson's disease bradykinesia may reflect an altered economic evaluation — unwillingness to expend effort for available reward.
- **Convergence on superior colliculus:** Cortical (excitatory) and basal ganglia (inhibitory) inputs converge, jointly determining where to saccade and how vigorously.

---

## Key Predictions and Open Questions

- How does vigor vary with risk, delay, and magnitude of reward?
- Is vigor affected by cognitive effort as well as physical effort?
- Is vigor truly trait-like? Do people who reach fast also walk fast and speak rapidly?
- Does a change in utility affect movements not relevant to the current task?
- How does vigor change with resource constraints like fatigue?
