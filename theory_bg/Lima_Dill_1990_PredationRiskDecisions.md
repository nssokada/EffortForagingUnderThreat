# Behavioral Decisions Under Predation Risk

**Lima, S. L. & Dill, L. M. (1990). Behavioral decisions made under the risk of predation: a review and prospectus. *Canadian Journal of Zoology*, 68, 619–640.**

---

## Central Thesis

Predation is not merely an evolutionary force shaping morphology — it is a **real-time influence on behavioral decision-making**. Animals assess and behaviorally modulate their risk of predation in ecological time (i.e., during their lifetime), and this assessment permeates virtually every behavioral domain: foraging, escape, sociality, reproduction, and even respiration.

---

## Components of Predation Risk

Lima & Dill decompose predation risk into a formal structure. The probability of being killed over some time period is:

**P(death) = 1 − exp(−αdT)**

where:

- **α** = encounter rate between predator and prey
- **d** = probability of death given an encounter
- **T** = time spent vulnerable to encounter or attack

These are the **basic components** of predation risk. The critical point is that each is *potentially assessable* by the prey and *at least partially under behavioral control*.

### Subcomponents of *d* (probability of death given encounter)

An encounter situation unfolds through a branching sequence of conditional probabilities (see their Figure 1):

1. Whether prey detects predator first, predator detects prey first, or no actual interaction occurs
2. Whether effective avoidance occurs (*a*)
3. Whether the predator ignores or attacks (*i₁*, *i₂*)
4. Whether the prey escapes before capture (*e₁*), or after capture (*e₃*)
5. Whether an unaware prey escapes (*e₂*)

Formally:

**d = [p(1 − a)(1 − i₁)(1 − e₁) + q(1 − i₂)(1 − e₂)](1 − e₃)**

where *p* = probability prey detects predator first, *q* = probability predator detects prey first.

### Assessability and Behavioral Control

The key insight: because these components are assessable, animals can **behaviorally control** their risk. Examples of control mechanisms:

- Reduce **T** by spending less time in exposed areas
- Reduce **α** by avoiding areas of high predator density
- Reduce **d** by feeding close to protective cover (improving escape probability *e₁*, *e₂*)
- Increase detection probability **p** through vigilance

---

## The Trade-off Framework

The paper's organizing principle: **decision-making reflects trade-offs between predation risk and the benefits of engaging in a given activity**. The animal cannot simultaneously maximize foraging gain and minimize predation risk — it must balance competing demands.

This trade-off is **not** a constraint. Lima & Dill explicitly argue against calling predation risk a "constraint" on foraging. The behavioral options lie on a continuum from pure energy maximization to pure risk minimization. Neither extreme is optimal; the animal chooses a point in between. True constraints are factors outside the animal's control (gut size, day length, feeding anatomy).

---

## Decision Hierarchy for Feeding Animals

Decisions are organized from large- to small-scale:

### When to Feed
- Temporal variation in predator activity creates windows of higher and lower risk
- Nocturnal foragers reduce activity in bright moonlight (encounter component)
- Diurnal foragers face higher risk during crepuscular periods (detection component)
- Resumption of feeding after predator sighting depends on dominance status and energetic need

### Where to Feed
- **Habitat/patch selection**: animals trade off foraging profitability against habitat-specific predator abundance (demonstrated across freshwater fish, invertebrates, birds, mammals)
- **Feeding-site selection within a patch**: proximity to protective cover is a major determinant (escape subcomponents *e₁*, *e₂*)
- Cover can function as both safety and a source of ambush attacks — creating a non-monotonic relationship between distance-to-cover and risk

### What to Eat
- Diet selection can deviate from classical optimal diet theory when items differ in the degree to which handling and vigilance are mutually exclusive
- Flock-size-dependent diet selection in juncos: larger flocks reduce the need for individual vigilance, shifting the diet toward more profitable but attention-demanding items

### How to Handle Food
- Handling times are under behavioral control, not fixed constraints
- Food-deprived birds scan less during handling (accepting higher predation risk for faster energy intake)
- Birds in the open break up food less before consumption, reducing exposed time at the cost of digestive efficiency

## Vigilance and Group Size

The **group size effect** on vigilance is one of the most robust findings: individual vigilance decreases with increasing group size, across mammals and birds. The underlying trade-off: vigilance detracts from energy intake, but more group members provide more eyes to detect predators.

Complications include:
- The benefit of being first to detect/respond to an attack
- Competition for depletable food
- Edge effects in flocks (Inglis & Lazarus 1981)
- False alarms (Dehn 1986)
- The object of vigilance (predators vs. conspecifics)
- Evolutionary stability of vigilance patterns

**Sentinels** — individuals that completely forego feeding to stand guard — raise additional questions about evolutionary stability and reciprocation.

---

## Escape Decisions

Flight-initiation distance (the distance at which an animal begins to flee) is predicted to increase with:
- Distance to safety (cover, burrow)
- Perceived lethality of the predator

And to decrease with:
- Group size (dilution, detection benefits)
- Crypticity of the prey

The decision to flee has costs (energy expenditure, lost opportunity), so it should depend on the animal's assessment of risk, not be automatic.

---

## Reproduction Under Predation Risk

A major gap identified: reproductive behavior has received almost no attention from a predation-risk decision-making perspective. Yet reproductive activities clearly increase predation risk (Tables 1 & 2 in the paper list numerous examples for both males and females).

Key questions proposed:
- **When** to reproduce: synchronous breeding may reduce per-capita risk
- **Where** to reproduce: nest-site and breeding-site selection based on local predation risk
- **How** to reproduce: clutch size, reproductive effort, and alternative mating tactics should all be sensitive to assessed predation risk

## Modeling Considerations

Most models incorporate predation via a **constant death rate** μ, so that P(death in time T) = 1 − exp(−μT). But μ = αd, and animals have behavioral control over both components. The death rate is not a habitat-specific constant — it is itself under behavioral control.

Lima & Dill call for models that:
- Consider the relevant components of risk
- Recognize that many components are under behavioral control of *both* prey and predator
- Use game-theoretic analyses where appropriate

## Perceptions and Problems

### How Is Risk Perceived?
- Some components are easily measured (distance to safety, time exposed)
- Others are not (probability of escape — direct sampling has obvious drawbacks)
- Animals likely use simple **heuristic rules** reflecting evolutionary history: e.g., "assume attack is likely until experience allows more detailed assessment"

### Methodological Concerns
- Animals may never perceive zero risk, even in predator-absent controls
- Observers may be perceived as predators
- Spatial scale of experiments matters — artificial proximity to predators may distort behavior
- Observed mortality rates may greatly underestimate *perceived* risk (the pedestrian-crossing-the-street analogy)
- Lack of observed predation does not imply lack of behavioral sensitivity to risk

---

## Key Takeaways

1. Predation risk is an **integral part** of decision-making, not a background constraint
2. The term "foraging behavior" is misleading insofar as it implies the animal is only considering food
3. Animals possess sophisticated abilities to **assess** and **behaviorally control** multiple components of predation risk
4. Decision-making reflects **trade-offs** between predation risk and the benefits of activity
5. Virtually every behavioral domain — foraging, sociality, escape, reproduction, respiration — is influenced by predation risk assessment
6. Future research should consider predation risk as a determinant of behavior **from the outset**
