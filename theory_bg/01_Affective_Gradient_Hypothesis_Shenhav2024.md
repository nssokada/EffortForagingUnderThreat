# The Affective Gradient Hypothesis (AGH)

**Source:** Shenhav, A. (2024). The affective gradient hypothesis: an affect-centered account of motivated behavior. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 28*(12), 1089–1104.

---

## Core Claim

Affect is the **only** form of value that drives motivated behavior. There is no need for a separate "cold" valuation system. All goal-directed and reflexive behavior emerges from the brain's process of traversing an affective gradient — moving toward states that feel better and away from states that feel worse.

---

## The Problem AGH Addresses

Traditional models of motivation and decision-making propose that:

- People compute "cold" scalar estimates of expected utility/reward.
- Feelings are secondary — either epiphenomena of value, learned inputs to value, incidental biases on value, or drivers of a separate primitive (Pavlovian) system.

All of these models assume that some form of value drives behavior **independent of feelings**. But this creates unresolved puzzles: How is value computed without feelings? How are feelings and values weighed against each other? How are goals selected and prioritized?

---

## Key Definitions

- **Affect (core affect):** A feeling that varies continuously along multiple dimensions (e.g., valence, arousal). Not limited to intense emotional episodes — includes subtle, fleeting, and "neutral" feelings. Not synonymous with discrete emotions (anger, sadness) but likely underpins them.
- **Affective features:** The affective qualities attached to any internally represented state (e.g., eating a meal, missing a deadline, getting a grant funded).
- **Gradient:** A set of continuous values defining the direction one needs to move within a space to achieve an objective.
- **Motivated behavior:** Actions driven by the value of expected outcomes, whether reflexive or deliberate.

---

## The Affect-Infused State Space: Five Principles

1. **Every representable state carries affective features.** Eating a meal, receiving criticism, paying bills — each has a specific affective identity (e.g., taste of a food) and scalar intensities along dimensions like valence and arousal.

2. **Affective features are evoked by experienced, recalled, or imagined states.** You feel something about a meal whether you're eating it now, remembering a past one, or imagining a future one.

3. **Affective features can be evoked bottom-up or top-down.** A poster for a restaurant triggers affect automatically (bottom-up); deliberately considering dinner options accesses the same affect through directed search (top-down).

4. **Multiple affective features can be evoked in parallel.** At any moment, you can simultaneously represent feelings about your current task, alternative activities, bodily states (hunger, pain), and social concerns.

5. **Salience scales with accessibility.** States that are more vivid, probable, immediate, or personally relevant evoke more salient affective features. Psychologically distant outcomes (improbable, far in the future) have weaker affective pull.

## The Affective Gradient: From Affect to Motivation

The landscape of affective features held in mind at any moment serves as a **guide for adjusting behavior**:

- **Attractive landmarks:** States expected to improve one's affect (approach them).
- **Repulsive landmarks:** States expected to worsen one's affect (avoid them).

Each possible action occupies a location along a multidimensional gradient, defined by the expected affective consequences of taking that action.

**The AGH proposes:** This gradient is the objective function for motivated behavior. Actions and control states are actively mobilized to traverse this gradient — maximizing expected positive affect and minimizing expected negative affect.

### Example: Grant Writing

A person working on a grant might simultaneously represent:
- Positive affect: satisfaction of submitting, elation if funded
- Negative affect: dread of missing a deadline, mental fatigue, hunger
- Alternative temptations: curiosity about an incoming email, desire to eat

The person persists with writing because the affective gradient (avoiding deadline failure, achieving submission satisfaction) currently outweighs the pull of alternatives.

## How AGH Differs from Traditional Models

| Feature | Traditional Models | AGH |
|---|---|---|
| What drives behavior | Cold value estimates, with affect as input/output | Affect alone |
| Hot vs. cold systems | Separate systems for emotional vs. rational evaluation | No cold evaluations exist; all evaluation is affective |
| Role of goals | Goals are explicit representations that direct behavior | Goals are emergent properties of currently accessible affective associations |
| Common currency | Values integrated into a single scalar for comparison | No common currency required; affective features influence actions directly and in parallel |
| Self-regulation conflicts | Competing "selves" (present vs. future, selfish vs. moral) | Competing affective consequences of one's actions (ambivalence) |

---

## The "Homungoalus" Problem and AGH's Solution

Traditional cognitive control models solved the "homunculus problem" (how does the controller know when to intervene?) by proposing goal-monitoring mechanisms. But this creates a deeper problem: **How does the person know which goal to pursue?**

AGH dissolves this problem by removing goals from their traditional explanatory role:

- Behavior is dynamically reconfigured based on whichever affective associations are currently accessible.
- A person continues their current task because abandoning it would produce more negative affect — but switches when a different activity's positive affect becomes more salient.
- Goals are not causes of behavior; they are descriptions of the pattern of affective priorities at a given moment.

---

## Broader Implications

### No Hot/Cold Distinction
Under AGH, there are no affect-free evaluations. Processes previously called "hot" vs. "cold" differ not in whether affect is involved but in **how** it is involved (e.g., intensity, arousal level). Identical outcomes should share overlapping neural codes whether accessed automatically or deliberately.

### Self-Regulation as Affective Competition
Self-regulation "failures" and "successes" depend on the relative strength and salience of competing affective representations — not on which "self" wins. For example: enjoying a dessert (positive) vs. fearing weight-related judgment (negative) vs. satisfaction of self-control (positive).

### No Common Currency Needed
AGH predicts that people cannot directly compare how two options feel in the abstract. Instead, options influence actions and control states directly and in parallel, achieving a form of multi-objective optimization without requiring conversion to a single value scale.

## Individual Differences and Clinical Implications

Understanding motivation requires knowing the **affective expectations** a person has about:
1. How performing an action feels (e.g., effort)
2. What results from that action (e.g., approval)
3. What results from inaction (e.g., reprimand)
4. What results from alternative actions (e.g., foregone opportunities)

Motivational disorders (apathy, addiction, impulsivity) can be understood as distortions in these affective expectations — not just as failures of willpower or goal-maintenance.

People also vary in their **affective goals** (e.g., some seek high-arousal positive states like elation; others seek low-arousal states like serenity), which act as a meta-parameter shaping the gradient's influence on behavior.

## Outstanding Questions

- How are affective features learned (integration vs. abstraction of current states)?
- Can motivated behavior persist without affective input (e.g., through habits or recurrence)?
- What defines the boundaries between states in the mental state space, and what determines accessibility?
- How is the many-to-many mapping between affective features and actions optimized neurally?
- Is the optimization objective to maximize along a particular affective axis, or to maintain an affective set-point?
