# Preregistered Hypotheses

## Effort Reallocation Under Threat: A Computational Account of Foraging Decisions and Motor Vigor

Okada, Garg, Wise, Mobbs

---

The overarching questions being asked are A) how do humans integrate effort cost and predation risk when making foraging decisions, and B) how does this integration extend from choice into motor execution and subjective experience?

We will test this using a virtual foraging task where subjects choose between food items varying in reward, effort, and predation risk, then physically execute their foraging bout by pressing keys under threat of capture.

Specific hypotheses are as follows:

1. Foraging choices will be best explained by a survival-weighted value model in which effort enters as an additive physical cost and survival probability separates attack likelihood from escape distance
    a. Additive effort will outperform multiplicative effort discounting
    b. A hyperbolic survival kernel will outperform an exponential kernel
    c. The combined effort-threat model will substantially outperform an effort-only baseline

2. The model-derived survival probability S will predict trial-level subjective affect
    a. Higher S will predict lower anxiety within subjects
    b. Higher S will predict higher confidence within subjects

3. Participants will deploy motor effort beyond task demands in proportion to danger, as captured by a positive population-mean danger mobilization parameter δ
    a. The posterior credible interval for μ_δ will exclude zero
    b. More than 80% of participants will show positive δ

4. Choice and vigor adjustments under threat will be coherently coupled, not independent
    a. Per-subject choice shift and vigor shift will be anti-correlated
    b. Threat bias in choice (β) will positively correlate with vigor mobilization (δ) across independently estimated Bayesian models
    c. Effort sensitivity (k) will negatively correlate with δ — representing an alternative coping strategy
    d. The reallocation strategy will approximate the expected-value-maximizing policy, with β and δ predicting greater optimality

5. Individuals who mobilize more vigor under danger will also show more accurate affective tracking of the survival signal
    a. δ will predict steeper within-subject anxiety slopes on S
    b. δ will predict steeper within-subject confidence slopes on S
    c. Higher δ will be associated with lower mean anxiety — adaptive calibration, not chronic anxiousness

6. The computational parameters governing effort reallocation will be orthogonal to self-reported psychiatric symptomatology
    a. No model parameter (k, β, δ) or their coupling will predict psychiatric factor scores (distress, fatigue, apathy) after correction for multiple comparisons
    b. Tonic baseline vigor (α) will uniquely predict the apathy/amotivation factor
