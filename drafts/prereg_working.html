1. Hypotheses

We test seven pre-specified hypotheses organized into four sections, derived from the exploratory sample (N=293). The computational framework estimates four per-subject parameters from independently fit hierarchical Bayesian models:

  • k — effort sensitivity (choice model)
  • β — threat bias (choice model)
  • α — baseline vigor (vigor model)
  • δ — danger mobilization (vigor model)
  • S — survival probability: S = (1 − T) + T/(1 + λD)
Section I

Threat will shift choice, vigor, and subjective experience

H1 — Threat shifts choice, vigor, and affect

Threat will reduce high-effort choice, amplify distance-driven avoidance, and increase excess motor effort — while shifting anxiety upward and confidence downward.
H1a — High-effort choice decreases with threat and distance

Primary tests:

  1. 3×3 RM-ANOVA (threat × distance) on P(choose high). Main effect of threat: F > 100 expl: F > 500
  2. Main effect of distance: F > 100 expl: F > 500
  3. Threat × distance interaction: F > 10, p < 0.001
H1b — Excess effort increases with threat
  1. RM-ANOVA on excess effort across threat levels: F > 5, p < 0.01
  2. Within constant-demand trials, excess effort still increases with threat: t > 2.0, p < 0.05 one-tailed
H1c — Affect shifts with threat
  1. RM-ANOVA: anxiety increases across threat levels. F > 50, p < 0.001
  2. RM-ANOVA: confidence decreases across threat levels. F > 50, p < 0.001
Support: All three sub-tests (H1a, H1b, H1c) must show predicted directional effects at p < 0.001.
Rejection: Any of H1a, H1b, or H1c fails to show the predicted directional effect.
Section II

Coherent coupling and optimality

H2 — Choice shift and vigor shift are coherently coupled

Participants who shift choices most toward safety under high threat will show the largest increase in excess effort.
H2a — Choice-vigor coupling across individuals

Pearson r(Δchoice, Δvigor) < 0, p < 0.01 one-tailed.

Where Δchoice = P(choose high | T=0.9) − P(choose high | T=0.1) and Δvigor = excess_effort(T=0.9) − excess_effort(T=0.1).

H2b — Split-half robustness

Pearson r(Δchoice_odd, Δvigor_even) < 0, p < 0.05 one-tailed. Same for reversed split.

Support: H2a must be significant. H2b provides robustness confirmation.
Rejection: The correlation in H2a is non-negative (r ≥ 0).

H3 — The reallocation strategy approximates the optimal policy

Participants who reallocate more will achieve higher foraging earnings. The dominant deviation from optimal will be excessive caution.
H3a — Reallocation predicts earnings

Pearson r(reallocation_index, total_earnings) > 0, p < 0.01 one-tailed.

Reallocation index = |Δchoice_i| + |Δvigor_i| (standardized).

H3b — Dominant deviation is excessive caution

Among suboptimal trials, proportion of "too cautious" errors exceeds 50%. One-sample t-test: t > 2.0, p < 0.05.

Support: Both H3a and H3b directionally consistent.
Rejection: Reallocation uncorrelated or negatively correlated with earnings.
Section III

A shared survival computation links choice and vigor

H4 — The survival-weighted additive-effort choice model best explains foraging behavior

Choice behavior is best explained by a model with (a) additive effort costs, (b) hyperbolic survival function, and (c) subject-specific threat bias β.
SV = R · S − k · E − β · (1 − S)
S  = (1 − T) + T / (1 + λ · D)

M1SV = R·exp(−kE)Does threat matter?
M2SV = R·exp(−kE) − β·T·DMechanistic S or linear features?
M3SV = R·exp(−kE)·S − β·(1−S), S=exp(−λTD)Which survival kernel?
M4SV = R·exp(−kE)·S − β·(1−S), S=(1−T)+T/(1+λD)Which effort structure?
M5SV = R·S − k·E − β·(1−S)Winner
H4a — Additive effort outperforms multiplicative

ΔELBO(M5 − M4) > 0 expl: +158

H4b — Hyperbolic survival outperforms exponential

ΔELBO(M4 − M3) > 0 expl: +174

Additional: ΔELBO(M5 − M1) > 100 expl: +2,038

H4c — Model-derived S predicts trial-level anxiety and confidence
  1. anxiety ~ S_probe_z + (1 + S_probe_z | subject) → β < 0, |t| > 3.0 expl: β = −0.605, t = −25.63
  2. confidence ~ S_probe_z + (1 + S_probe_z | subject) → β > 0, |t| > 3.0 expl: β = +0.612, t = +25.65

Secondary: k → mean anxiety (positive) and k → mean confidence (negative), OLS, p < 0.05 one-tailed.

Support: H4a + H4b directionally consistent; both H4c primary tests significant.
Rejection: Multiplicative or exponential outperforms winner, or H4c betas wrong direction.

H5 — Model-derived danger drives excess motor vigor

Lower survival S causes participants to press harder, expressed as positive population-mean δ in a hierarchical Bayesian model.
excess_ij = α_i + δ_i · (1 − S_ij) + ε_ij
ε_ij ~ Normal(0, σ)
H5a — Population-mean danger mobilization is positive
  1. P(μ_δ > 0 | data) > 0.975 (95% one-sided credibility) expl: P = 1.0
  2. Proportion of subjects with δ_i > 0 must exceed 80% expl: 98.3%

Secondary: σ_δ > 0.05 expl: σ_δ = 0.146

Support: Both primary tests met.
Rejection: μ_δ posterior includes zero, or <65% subjects show δ > 0.

H6 — Choice and vigor parameters are coupled across independent models

β (threat bias) and δ (danger mobilization) are positively correlated; k (effort sensitivity) and δ are negatively correlated — from models sharing no parameters or data.
H6a — β-δ and k-δ couplings
  1. r(log(β), δ) > 0, p < .001 one-tailed expl: r = +0.53
  2. r(log(k), δ) < 0, p < .01 one-tailed expl: r = −0.33
H6b — Joint model CIs exclude zero

Joint LKJ model: ρ(β,δ) > 0 and ρ(k,δ) < 0, both with 95% CI excluding zero.

H6c — β and δ jointly predict optimality

OLS: optimality ~ β_z + δ_z + k_z. Both β and δ significant predictors (p < 0.05).

Support: Both H6a primary tests met.
Rejection: r(log(β), δ) ≤ 0 or r(log(k), δ) ≥ 0.
Section IV

Vigor mobilization predicts metacognitive accuracy

H7 — Individuals who mobilize vigor under danger show more accurate threat appraisal

Higher δ predicts tighter affective tracking of S. β shows the same pattern, but k does not — dissociating threat-responsive from effort-cost parameters.
H7a — δ predicts affect-S slopes
  1. r(δ, anxiety slope on S) < 0, p < .05 one-tailed expl: r = −0.311
  2. r(δ, confidence slope on S) > 0, p < .05 one-tailed expl: r = +0.325

Secondary: r(δ, mean anxiety) < 0 expl: r = −0.194

H7b — Dissociation: β predicts, k does not
  1. r(β, anxiety slope on S) < 0, p < .05 one-tailed
  2. r(k, anxiety slope on S) is non-significant (p > .05, two-tailed)
Support: Both H7a tests significant. H7b shows dissociation (β significant, k not).

2. Study Design

Participants

Confirmatory sample: N = 350 via Prolific (desktop only). ~40–50 min sessions. $10/hr base + performance bonuses.

Blinding: Preregistration locked on AsPredicted before confirmatory data are opened.

Task Design

Virtual foraging task (Unity WebGL). Circular arena with safe zone at center. Cookies at radial distances D ∈ {1, 2, 3}.

  • Options: Low-effort (E=0.4, D=1, R=1) vs high-effort (E∈{0.6,0.8,1.0}, D∈{1,2,3}, R=5)
  • Effort: Press S+D+F keys → movement speed (≥100%/≥50%/≥25%/<25% = full/half/quarter/zero)
  • Threat: T ∈ {0.1, 0.5, 0.9}. Predator approaches slowly, then strikes at 4× player speed
  • Penalties: Capture = lose cookie reward + 5 points
  • Structure: 3 blocks × 27 trials = 81 events (45 choice + 36 probe)
  • Probes: Anxiety / confidence ratings (0–7) on forced-choice trials
  • Psychiatric battery: DASS-21, PHQ-9, OASIS, STAI-T, AMI, MFIS, STICSA

Sample Size Justification

N=350 matches planned design. Expected N ≈ 280–330 after exclusions. Weakest test (H2a, moderate r) requires N > 200 for power > 0.95 at α = 0.01. All other tests based on very large effects (d > 1.5, t > 25, P(δ>0) = 1.0).

3. Analysis Plan

3.0 General Principles

  1. Same code, same parameters. Scripts from exploratory applied without modification (except file paths). Population parameters re-estimated.
  2. Sequential procedure: Preprocessing → H1 descriptives → H4 choice model (→ λ,k,β) → H4c affect → H5 vigor HBM (→ α,δ) → H2,H3,H6 coupling → H7 metacognition.
  3. No peeking. Preregistration locked before data opened.
  4. Thresholds: α = 0.05 two-tailed unless specified. FDR (BH) within each hypothesis family. Bayesian: 95% CI excluding zero.

3.1 Preprocessing

5-stage pipeline (scripts/preprocessing/pipeline.py): parse JSON → score questionnaires → extract keypresses → apply exclusions → merge files.

Outputs: behavior.csv, feelings.csv, psych.csv, subject_mapping.csv.

3.2 H1 — Behavioral Descriptives

H1a: 3×3 RM-ANOVA on P(choose high) per cell. Pairwise: T=0.1 vs T=0.9; D=1 vs D=3.

H1b: Per-subject excess effort by threat level → RM-ANOVA. Within constant-demand: subset + paired t-test.

H1c: Per-subject mean anxiety/confidence by threat → RM-ANOVA. Pairwise T=0.1 vs T=0.9.

3.3 H4 — Choice Model Comparison & Affect

Inference: SVI (NumPyro, Adam lr=0.01, 30K steps) and/or MCMC (NUTS, 4×1000+1000, Rhat ≤ 1.05).

Fixed constants: R_H=5, R_L=1, C=5, E_L=0.4.

H4a/H4b: ΔELBO comparisons. H4c: LMMs via statsmodels REML. S_probe uses only population λ.

3.4 H5 — Vigor HBM

NumPyro NUTS (4 chains, target_accept=0.90) or SVI. λ fixed from choice model. Probe trials excluded.

3.5 H2, H3, H6 — Coupling, Optimality, Cross-Model

H2: Pearson r(Δchoice, Δvigor). Split-half robustness.

H3: Reallocation index vs earnings. Optimal policy from known task parameters.

H6: r(log(β),δ) and r(log(k),δ). Joint LKJ model. Optimality regression.

3.6 H7 — Metacognitive Accuracy

Per-subject affect-S slopes → correlate with δ, β, k.

3.7 Quadrant Analysis (Pre-specified Descriptive)

Median splits on P(choose high) × α_bayes → HH/HL/LH/LL. k ANOVA, escape rate ANOVA, off-diagonal β comparison.

5. Exclusion Criteria

  1. Task completion: All 81 trials completed
  2. Valid calibration: ≥10 presses in calibration
  3. Valid keypresses: No automated input (>3 SD) or disengagement (>50% zero-press trials)
  4. Valid predator dynamics: <10% physically impossible trials
  5. Task engagement: Escape rate ≥35%
  6. Probe validity: ≥80% probe completion (≥29/36). Excluded from H4c/H7 only.

6. Sample Size Justification

N=350 matches planned design. Expected 280–330 after exclusions. Weakest test (H2a) powered at >0.95 for r=0.20 at α=0.01. All H1/H4/H5 based on massive effects. H4c LMM power >0.999 even at 50% effect reduction.

8. Inference Approach

LMMs: statsmodels REML. Model comparison: ΔELBO (NumPyro SVI). Bayesian HBMs: NumPyro NUTS (Rhat, ESS). FDR (BH) within hypothesis families. Effect sizes: Cohen's d, partial η², marginal/conditional R².

Appendix A — Model Equations

Winning choice model (M5)

S_o(T, D_o) = (1 − T) + T / (1 + λ · D_o)
SV_o = R_o · S_o − k_i · E_o − β_i · (1 − S_o)
p(choose H) = σ(τ · (SV_H − SV_L))

Priors:
  log k_i ~ Normal(μ_k, σ_k);  μ_k ~ Normal(0,1);  σ_k ~ HalfNormal(1)
  log β_i ~ Normal(μ_β, σ_β);  μ_β ~ Normal(0,1);  σ_β ~ HalfNormal(1)
  λ ~ LogNormal(0,1);  τ ~ LogNormal(0,1)

Vigor HBM

excess_ij = α_i + δ_i · (1 − S_ij) + ε_ij,  ε ~ Normal(0, σ)

Priors:
  α_i ~ Normal(μ_α, σ_α);  δ_i ~ Normal(μ_δ, σ_δ)
  μ_α, μ_δ ~ Normal(0,1);  σ_α, σ_δ, σ ~ HalfNormal(1)

Joint model (H6b robustness)

[log(k_i), log(β_i), α_i, δ_i] ~ MVN(μ, Σ)
Σ = diag(σ) · Ω · diag(σ),  Ω ~ LKJCholesky(η = 2)
