# Draft 002: Deciding and Doing Dissociate Under Threat

## Title
"Deciding and doing dissociate under threat: metacognitive miscalibration between effort-based choice and motor vigor during human foraging"

## Abstract

When humans forage under predation threat, what they choose and how hard they press are governed by independent computational processes. An effort-discounting parameter (c_effort) determines foraging decisions, while tonic motor engagement (alpha) determines pressing vigor — and these are uncorrelated (r ≈ 0). This dissociation has three consequences. First, vigor determines survival (3.6× escape difference within the same choice group), yet subjective confidence tracks choice rather than vigor, creating systematic metacognitive miscalibration. Second, a Simpson's paradox hides threat-driven vigor modulation: vigor appears flat across threat levels, but conditioning on choice reveals robust increases (d = 0.42–0.49). Third, the calibration of subjective anxiety to objective danger predicts task performance (r = .23) but is unrelated to clinical symptoms, while excess anxiety (discrepancy) predicts clinical measures (r = .21–.31) but not performance — a double dissociation linking metacognition to both adaptive behavior and psychopathology.

## Results Structure

### R1: A cost-benefit model captures foraging decisions under threat

The choice model: SV = R·S − c_effort·E − c_death·(1−S), with binary survival logic (must press at full speed to escape). Distance enters effort cost, not survival (predator spawn calibrated to return time).

Key results:
- 82% choice accuracy, r = 0.99 across 9 conditions
- c_effort and c_death independently identified (r ≈ 0)
- c_effort maps to task confidence (r = −0.24, p < .0001)
- Correctly derives optimal policy: heavy at T=0.1, light at T≥0.5

### R2: Motor vigor is independent of the choice computation but determines survival

Vigor (pressing rate) is a stable individual trait — 26% between-subject variance, only 4% condition-driven. Alpha (tonic vigor) does NOT enter the choice model (model comparison rejects it).

The dissociation has survival consequences:
- Within the same choice group, high-alpha subjects escape 3.6× more often
- Choosing the hard cookie actually HURTS survival (farther from safety)
- Vigor, not choice, determines who lives

**Simpson's Paradox:** Vigor appears flat across threat (d = 0.28 marginal). But conditioning on chosen cookie reveals robust threat modulation (heavy d = 0.42, light d = 0.49). The null is an artifact of choice reallocation masking vigor increases.

### R3: Confidence tracks choice, not vigor — systematic metacognitive miscalibration

People's subjective confidence is miscalibrated:
- Confidence tracks choice quality (which cookie they picked) not motor quality (how hard they pressed)
- But survival depends on motor quality, not choice quality
- This creates a population of "overconfident" subjects who choose ambitiously but press weakly
- And "underconfident" subjects who choose cautiously but press hard — these are the highest survivors

The anxiety-confidence dissociation: within-subject r = −0.25 (not mirror images). 28% of subjects show positive coupling (more anxious AND more confident).

### R4: Anxiety calibration and discrepancy doubly dissociate — linking metacognition to performance and psychopathology

**Calibration** (how well anxiety tracks threat): predicts performance (r = .23 with earnings, r = .19 with escapes) but NOT clinical symptoms (all p > .4).

**Discrepancy** (excess anxiety relative to objective danger): predicts clinical anxiety (STAI-State r = .31, STICSA r = .27, DASS-Anxiety r = .25, PHQ-9 r = .21) but NOT performance.

This double dissociation means:
- You can be anxious AND well-calibrated → good performance + clinical symptoms
- You can be calm AND poorly-calibrated → poor performance + no symptoms
- Calibration and level are orthogonal dimensions of threat processing

**The apathy paradox:** Higher self-reported apathy (AMI) predicts BETTER task performance (r = .29-.33 with earnings, escapes, effort). "Apathetic" individuals may be adopting a conservative-but-capable strategy that clinical instruments mischaracterize as amotivation.

### R5: Anxiety sensitization as a clinical marker

Anxiety increases over the session for some subjects (sensitizers). Sensitization predicts:
- STAI-State (r = .22, p = .0001)
- PHQ-9 depression (r = .18, p = .001)
- DASS-Depression (r = .14, p = .017)

Failure to habituate to repeated threat exposure may index vulnerability to anxiety and depression.

## Why This Is Nature Comms

1. **Challenges a core assumption.** The field assumes effort-based decisions and effort-based actions share a common value signal. We show they don't.

2. **Simpson's paradox is a methodological contribution.** Any study examining effort under threat that doesn't condition on choice will conclude vigor is threat-insensitive. This affects interpretation of prior work.

3. **The metacognition bridge.** Confidence miscalibration and the calibration-discrepancy dissociation connect computational neuroscience to clinical psychology. This is the translational hook.

4. **The apathy paradox.** Clinical apathy instruments may mischaracterize a high-performing subgroup. Implications for clinical assessment and trial design.

5. **Preregistered + confirmatory.** Run on exploratory (N=293), confirm on preregistered sample (N=350).
