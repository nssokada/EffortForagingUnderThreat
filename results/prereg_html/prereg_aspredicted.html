Hypothesis

The overarching questions being asked are A) how do humans integrate energetic cost and predation risk when foraging, and B) does this integration manifest coherently across choice behavior, motor execution, and subjective experience?

We will test this using a task where subjects forage for food items in a virtual environment under threat of capture by a virtual predator, choosing between options that vary in reward, effort cost, and escape distance, then physically executing their foraging bout by pressing keys.

The computational framework estimates four per-subject parameters from independently fit hierarchical Bayesian models:

Parameter Name Level Description
$k$ Effort discounting Subject Fixed cost of choosing the high-effort option (from choice model). Effort enters as binary indicator ($E = 1$ for high, $E = 0$ for low), forcing distance to enter exclusively through the survival function.
$\beta$ Threat bias Subject How strongly danger deters choice beyond expected value (from choice model).
$\alpha$ Baseline vigor Subject Tonic pressing rate above task demand (from vigor model).
$\delta$ Danger mobilization Subject How much excess effort increases with model-derived danger, $1 - S$ (from vigor model).
$\gamma$ Effort demand constraint Population Population-level adjustment for physical demand of the chosen option (from vigor model).
$S$ Survival probability Function $S = (1 - T) + T/(1 + \lambda D)$, integrating threat probability $T$ and distance $D$ (shared across models; exploratory $\lambda \approx 0.70$).

Hypotheses

H1 — Threat shifts choice, effort, and affect
Hypothesis: Threat will reduce high-effort choice, increase excess motor effort, and shift subjective anxiety upward and confidence downward.
H1a — High-effort choice will decrease with threat probability and with escape distance. Logistic LMM: choice ~ threat_z + dist_z + threat_z:dist_z + (1|subj). All $\beta < 0$, $p < .01$, plus monotonicity.
H1b — Excess effort will increase with threat, including within constant-demand trials. Linear LMM: excess ~ threat_z × dist_z + effort_chosen_z + (1|subj). $\beta(\text{threat}) > 0$; $\beta(\text{threat} \times \text{dist}) < 0$.
H1c — Trial-level anxiety will increase and confidence will decrease with threat probability. LMMs with random slopes: $\beta > 0$ for anxiety, $\beta < 0$ for confidence, both $p < .001$.
H2 — Choice–vigor coupling
Hypothesis: Choice shift and vigor shift under threat will be coherently coupled across individuals.
H2a — Participants who shift choices most toward safety will show the largest increase in excess effort. Pearson $r(\Delta\text{choice}, \Delta\text{vigor}) < 0$, $p < .01$ one-tailed.
H2b — This coupling will remain significant when computed from independent trial halves (odd vs. even). $r < 0$, $p < .05$.
H3 — Near-optimal reallocation
Hypothesis: The reallocation strategy will approximate the expected-value-maximizing policy.
H3a — Participants who reallocate more will achieve higher foraging earnings. Pearson $r(\text{reallocation}, \text{earnings}) > 0$, $p < .01$ one-tailed.
H3b — The dominant deviation from optimal will be excessive caution rather than excessive risk-taking. $> 50\%$ of errors "too cautious", $t$-test $p < .05$.
H4 — Choice model comparison
Hypothesis: Choices will be best explained by a model in which effort enters as an additive physical cost and survival probability follows a hyperbolic function of distance.
H4a — Additive effort (M5) will outperform multiplicative effort (M4): $\Delta$ELBO $> 0$.
H4b — Hyperbolic survival (M4) will outperform exponential survival (M3): $\Delta$ELBO $> 0$.
H4c — Model-derived $S$ will predict trial-level anxiety ($\beta < 0$, $|t| > 3$) and confidence ($\beta > 0$, $|t| > 3$).
H5 — Danger-driven vigor
Hypothesis: Model-derived danger ($1 - S$) will drive excess motor effort at the population level, after controlling for effort demand of the chosen option, with meaningful individual variation.
H5a — Population-mean $\delta$ will be positive: $P(\mu_\delta > 0 \mid \text{data}) > 0.975$, and $> 80\%$ of subjects have $\delta_i > 0$.
H6 — Cross-model parameter coupling
Hypothesis: Computational parameters governing the effort-danger trade-off will covary across independently estimated models.
H6a — Pearson $r(\log(\beta), \delta) > 0$, $p < .001$ one-tailed (from independently fit models).
H6b — $\rho(\beta, \delta)$ posterior 95% CI $> 0$ in a joint LKJ model.
H6c — OLS: $\text{optimality} \sim \beta_z + \delta_z + k_z$. Both $\beta$ and $\delta$ significant ($p < .05$).
H7 — Metacognitive–motor bridge
Hypothesis: Participants whose motor effort is more danger-responsive (higher $\delta$) will show more accurate subjective threat appraisal.
H7a — $\delta$ will predict steeper within-subject anxiety slopes on $S$ (more negative) and confidence slopes on $S$ (more positive). $r(\delta, \text{anxiety slope}) < 0$, $r(\delta, \text{confidence slope}) > 0$, $p < .05$ one-tailed.
H7b — $\beta$ will show the same pattern, but effort sensitivity $k$ will not — dissociating threat-responsive parameters from effort cost sensitivity. $r(k, \text{anxiety slope})$ must be non-significant ($p > .05$, two-tailed).

Dependent Variables

  1. H1: (a) Binary choice of high- vs. low-effort cookie on each of 45 behavioral trials. (b) Excess effort: actual pressing rate minus effort demand of chosen option (proportion-of-capacity units). (c) Self-reported anxiety and confidence ratings on probe trials (0–7 scale), 18 per type per subject.
  2. H2: Per-subject shift scores: $\Delta\text{choice} = P(\text{high} \mid T{=}0.9) - P(\text{high} \mid T{=}0.1)$ and $\Delta\text{vigor} = \text{mean excess at } T{=}0.9 - \text{mean excess at } T{=}0.1$.
  3. H3: (a) Total foraging earnings and reallocation index $= |\Delta\text{choice}| + |\Delta\text{vigor}|$ (standardized). (b) Trial-level classification as "too cautious" or "too risky."
  4. H4: (a, b) Model fit by ELBO across 5 models. (c) Fixed-effect coefficient from LMM of probe ratings on $S_{\text{probe}}$.
  5. H5: Subject-level posterior mean $\delta_i$ and population-level $\mu_\delta$.
  6. H6: Pearson $r(\log(\beta), \delta)$ from independently fit models.
  7. H7: Per-subject within-subject regression slopes of affect on $S_{\text{probe}}$, correlated with $\delta$, $\beta$, $k$.

Conditions

One experiment with a within-subjects design. Each subject completes 81 trials (3 blocks × 27 trials) in a virtual foraging task. Conditions are fully crossed within subject:

  • Threat probability: $T \in \{0.1, 0.5, 0.9\}$
  • Distance: $D \in \{1, 2, 3\}$ corresponding to 5, 7, 9 game units from the safe zone
  • Effort demand: $E_H \in \{0.6, 0.8, 1.0\}$ of calibrated pressing capacity

The low-effort option is fixed ($E_L = 0.4$, $D_L = 1$, $R_L = 1$ pt). The high-effort option yields $R_H = 5$ pts. Capture penalty $C = 5$ pts + loss of current cookie reward.

Each block contains 15 regular behavioral trials and 12 probe trials (6 anxiety, 6 confidence). Probe trials use forced-choice (identical options) and collect ratings before pressing begins. A psychiatric battery (DASS-21, PHQ-9, OASIS, STAI-T, AMI, MFIS, STICSA) is administered between blocks.

Analyses

H1.

  1. Logistic mixed-effects model: choice ~ threat_z + dist_z + threat_z:dist_z + (1 | subject). Tests: $\beta(\text{threat}) < 0$, $\beta(\text{dist}) < 0$, $\beta(\text{threat} \times \text{dist}) < 0$. All $p < .01$. Plus monotonicity: $P(T{=}0.1) > P(T{=}0.5) > P(T{=}0.9)$ at each $D$, by paired $t$-tests ($p < .01$, one-tailed).
  2. Linear LMM: excess ~ threat_z * dist_z + effort_chosen_z + (1 | subject). Tests: $\beta(\text{threat}) > 0$, $p < .05$; $\beta(\text{threat} \times \text{dist}) < 0$, $p < .05$.
  3. Linear LMMs on probe ratings: anxiety ~ threat_z + dist_z + (1 + threat_z | subject) and same for confidence. Tests: $\beta(\text{threat}) > 0$ (anxiety), $\beta(\text{threat}) < 0$ (confidence), both $p < .001$. Estimated via Laplace approximation (H1a) and REML (H1b, H1c).

H2.

  1. Pearson $r(\Delta\text{choice}, \Delta\text{vigor}) < 0$, one-tailed, $p < .01$.
  2. Split-half: $r(\Delta\text{choice}_{\text{odd}}, \Delta\text{vigor}_{\text{even}}) < 0$, $p < .05$.

H3.

  1. Pearson $r(\text{reallocation}, \text{earnings}) > 0$, one-tailed, $p < .01$.
  2. Proportion "too cautious" $> 50\%$. One-sample $t$-test, $p < .05$.

H4.

  1. Five SVI models (binary effort): M1 effort-only ($SV = R \cdot e^{-kE}$), M2 linear features ($SV = R \cdot e^{-kE} - \beta \cdot T \cdot D$), M3 exponential survival ($SV = R \cdot e^{-kE} \cdot S_{\text{exp}} - \beta(1-S)$), M4 hyperbolic survival ($SV = R \cdot e^{-kE} \cdot S_{\text{hyp}} - \beta(1-S)$), M5 additive effort ($SV = R \cdot S - k \cdot E - \beta(1-S)$). Primary: M5 $>$ M4 $>$ M3 by $\Delta$ELBO. MCMC primary if convergence met ($\hat{R} \leq 1.05$, ESS $\geq 100$).
  2. LMM: affect ~ S_probe_z + (1 + S_probe_z | subject). $\beta < 0$ (anxiety), $\beta > 0$ (confidence), $|t| > 3$.

H5.

  1. Hierarchical Bayesian: $\text{excess}_{ij} = \alpha_i + \delta_i \cdot (1 - S_{ij}) + \gamma \cdot E_{\text{chosen},ij} + \varepsilon_{ij}$. Test: $P(\mu_\delta > 0 \mid \text{data}) > 0.975$, $> 80\%$ of $\delta_i > 0$.

H6.

  1. Pearson $r(\log(\beta), \delta) > 0$, $p < .001$ one-tailed.
  2. Joint LKJ model: $[\log(k_i), \log(\beta_i), \alpha_i, \delta_i] \sim \text{MVN}(\mu, \Sigma)$, $\Omega \sim \text{LKJCholesky}(\eta{=}2)$. Test: $\rho(\beta,\delta)$ 95% CI $> 0$.
  3. OLS: optimality ~ beta_z + delta_z + k_z. Both $\beta$ and $\delta$ significant ($p < .05$).

H7.

  1. Within-subject slopes of affect on $S_{\text{probe},z}$. Pearson $r(\delta, \text{anxiety slope}) < 0$, $r(\delta, \text{confidence slope}) > 0$, $p < .05$ one-tailed.
  2. $r(\beta, \text{anxiety slope}) < 0$, $p < .05$ one-tailed. $r(k, \text{anxiety slope})$ non-significant ($p > .05$, two-tailed).

Outliers and Exclusions

Exclusion criteria applied before any model fitting:

  1. Incomplete task (did not finish all 81 trials)
  2. Invalid calibration (fewer than 10 presses across calibration trials)
  3. Implausible keypresses: max single-trial press rate $> 3$ SD above sample mean (automated input), or zero presses on $> 50\%$ of regular trials (disengagement)
  4. Invalid predator dynamics: $> 10\%$ of trials with physically impossible predator behavior
  5. Low engagement: overall escape rate $< 35\%$ across attack trials
  6. Insufficient probes: $< 80\%$ probe completion ($< 29/36$). These subjects excluded from affect analyses (H4c, H7) only.

No post-hoc exclusions based on model fit quality or statistical extremity.

Sample Size

$N = 350$ recruited via Prolific. Expected $N \approx 280$–$330$ after exclusions (exploratory retention: 83.7%). The weakest powered test is the $\beta$-$\delta$ coupling (H6a, exploratory $r = +0.279$); detecting $r = 0.20$ at $\alpha = 0.001$ one-tailed requires $N > 200$ (power $> 0.90$). All other tests are based on large exploratory effects (choice: $d > 1.5$; affect LMM: $t > 25$; vigor: 96.6% $\delta > 0$; choice-vigor coupling $r \approx 0.2$–$0.3$).

Other

The exploratory sample ($N = 293$) was used to develop all hypotheses and specify all analysis plans. The confirmatory sample has been collected but not analyzed. The preregistration will be timestamped on AsPredicted before the confirmatory data are opened. All analysis code will be shared on GitHub and data on OSF upon acceptance.
