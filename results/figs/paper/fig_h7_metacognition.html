H7 — Individuals who mobilize vigor under danger show more accurate subjective threat appraisal

Figure. (A) Participants with higher danger mobilization ($\delta$) show steeper negative anxiety–$S$ slopes ($r = -0.311$), indicating their anxiety ratings more accurately track survival probability (H7a). (B) The same pattern for confidence: higher $\delta$ predicts steeper positive confidence–$S$ slopes ($r = +0.325$; H7a). (C) Dissociation across model parameters (H7b): threat-responsive parameters ($\delta$, $\beta$) predict calibration (***$p_{\text{FDR}} < .001$), while baseline vigor ($\alpha$) does not. Effort sensitivity ($k$) shows a weaker, opposite-signed effect. $N = 293$.

Statement: Participants whose motor effort is more danger-responsive (higher $\delta$) will show tighter affective tracking of survival probability $S$ — steeper anxiety–$S$ and confidence–$S$ slopes — constituting more accurate metacognitive appraisal of threat.
H7a.1 — $r(\delta,\, \text{anxiety slope on } S) < 0$, $p < .05$ one-tailed.
H7a.2 — $r(\delta,\, \text{confidence slope on } S) > 0$, $p < .05$ one-tailed.
H7a.3 (secondary) — $r(\delta,\, \text{mean anxiety}) < 0$: high-$\delta$ individuals report lower average anxiety despite stronger coupling.
H7b.1 — $\beta$ also predicts anxiety–$S$ slope ($r < 0$, $p < .05$).
H7b.2 — $k$ does not predict anxiety–$S$ slope ($p > .05$, two-tailed) — dissociating threat-responsive from effort-cost parameters.
